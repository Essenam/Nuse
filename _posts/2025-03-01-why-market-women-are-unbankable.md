Every morning, thousands of women set up their stalls in the markets of Lomé. They negotiate in Ewe, count change in CFA francs, and run businesses that feed their families and communities.

They are not poor. They are not financially illiterate. They are simply invisible to the institutions that could help them grow.

## The problem isn't the women. It's the data.

When a loan officer at FUCEC or Assilassimé evaluates a loan application, they need evidence of financial behavior. Bank statements. Transaction records. A credit history.

A market woman who has never had a bank account has none of these. Not because her business isn't viable — but because the system was never built for her.

> She has been running her business for fifteen years. She knows her costs, her margins, her slow seasons. But she has no way to prove it.

That's the gap Nuse was built to close.

## What 90 days of voice data can do

When a woman uses Nuse for 90 days, she builds something she has never had before: a verified financial record. Not a self-reported survey. Not an estimate. A real log of real transactions, recorded in her own voice, in her own language.

From that record, we can calculate:

- Her average monthly revenue
- Her consistency — does she record every day, or disappear for weeks?
- Her expense patterns and profit margins
- A credit readiness score: 0 to 100

That score is the bridge. It transforms 90 days of her behavior into a number a loan officer can act on.
